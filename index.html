Proposition: The NYPD is held adequately accountable for civilian complaints

FOR the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • [2] Data Transformations: The dataset was grouped by the year in which each complaint was received, and the total number of unique complaints (complaint_id) were calculated for each year. The number of distinct officers (unique_mos_id) involved in complaints during the same period was then determined. Dividing the total number of complaints by the number of officers each year produced the average number complaints per officer, providing a normalized measure that accounts for fluctuations in the size of the police force over time.
  • [2] Downward Trend Line: A linear trend line was added to highlight the overall decline in average complaints per officer over time, drawing attention to the long-term pattern rather than year-to-year fluctuations.
  • [-1] Truncated Y-Axis: The y-axis begins at 0.8 instead of 0 to visually accentuate the decrease in complaint rates. This design choice makes the downard trend appear steeper, enhancing the visual impression of improvement over time.
  • [2] Dropped Values: Data from 2020 was omitted due to incomplete records, as complaints were collected only during the early months of that year. Including this partial data would have created a misleading spike in the average complaints per officer. Removing it ensures the visualization maintains a consistent comparison across years.
  • [2] Annotations: Text labels were added to mark the peak and lowest points of the trend. These annotations guide viewers toward the most significant moments in the data, reinforcing the main proposition that the NYPD is held accountable for civilian complaints.

AGAINST the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • [2] Dropped 2020 Values: The year 2020 was intentionally excluded from the visualization because complaint data from that year is incomplete, likely as a result of the ongoing reporting period. Including data from that year would have introduced inaccuracies and falsely suggest a sharp decline in both complaints and substantiation rate. By removing this partial data, the visualization maintains a more consistent and fair representation of trends over time.
  • [-2] Framing bias through the title and interpretation: The visualization's title, "Officers Accused of Using Force Rarely Face Consequences," frames the data and conclusion in a negative light. It looks like there is a direct casual relationship between the number of force-related complaints and a low substantiation rate. While persuasive, this framing overlooks that the total number of substantiated comlpaints has increased over time. Moreover, around 1997, there were roughly 30 complaints * 0.4 substantiation rate = 12 substantiated complaints, whereas by 2015, approximately 550 complaints * 0.7 substation rate = 385 substantiated complaints. By focusing only on the ratio rather than the total substantiated outcomes, the visualization makes it seem as though accountability has declined. However, in reality, the number of officers facing consequences has increased, reavealing a deceptive framing that supports the opposing argument.
  • [-1] Selective Framing: The visualization focuses on complaints categorized under "fado_type" == force, ignoring all other possible categories. This selective inclusino amplifies the perception that the NYPD is not held accountable, as it highlightsa specific area of concern. By filtering the data in this way, the visualization limits the viewer's understanding of overll accountability trends across the department. If other complaint types were included, the substantiation rate may appear more balanced, potentially altering the narrative.
  • [-2] Dual-axis Manipulation and Misleading Color Coding: The visualization uses two y-axes (Red for total complaints, blue for substantiation rate) to suggest a relationship between metrics that are not directly comparable. The shared area between the two lines creates the illusion of a growing "gap" between complaints and accountability, as if the difference were a meaningul measure. However, the two variables are plotted on unrelated scales, so the shaded region does not represent any real quantity. This design choice misleads viewers into believing that accountability has declined over time, when the visual difference is merely a result of axes manipulation.

Final Reflection

This project examines whether the NYPD is held adequately accountable for civilian complaints. The supporting visualization indicate the NYPD officer behavior has moved in a positive direction, reflecting a genuine process in NYPD's accountability. In contrast, the opposing visualization suggests that while the total number of use-of-force complaints (red line) surged after 2002, the substantiation rate for these complaints (blue line) did not increase along with it. In fact, the rate is far lower than its peak in the late 1980s and early 1990s. This gap highlights the decreasing accountability of the police force. Both viewpoints don't have a clear right or wrong answer, as they each present valid interpretations of the data. It depends on how the viewer chooses to frame the issue of police accountability.

One surprising aspect of this project was how different data transformations could completely change how trends appeared. For instance, calculating ratios, such as the substantiation rate of force complaints, made long-term patterns much clearer and highlighted how the issue of excessive force has persisted over time. We also discovered that visual form plays a major role in persuasion: combining a line chart with a regression line often produced the most convincing narrative. By emphasizing the overall downward or upward trend, these visuals helped audiences focus on the broader pattern rather than minor year-to-year fluctuations. At the same time, creating persuasive yet ethical visualizations proved to be a challenging balance. While it was relatively easy to compute yearly averages using groupby functions and generate line plots, finding data that convincingly supported either side of our proposition required extensive exploration. In many cases, the data did not align with our initial expectations causing many road bumps, forcing us to rethink how to best represent it. The most difficult aspect was maintaining analytical depth and clarity without oversimplifying complex patterns or creating misleading narratives through poor design choices. Subtle visual techniques, such as axis scaling or selective filtering, could make trends appear more dramatic than they truly were, reminding us how easily data can be manipulated to fit a desired story.

Through this process, we came to define ethical analysis and visualization as the effort to present data truthfully, without ignoring unfavorable information or reinforcing bias. Ethical visualization means helping audiences correctly interpret the data's meaning without being influenced by misleading design choices. Techniques like truncating axes, using emotionally charged titles, or selectively filtering data can all skew perception, so maintaining transparency about these decisions is essential to establish trust. An ethical visualization should reveal the full context behind the data, acknowledging its limitations and potential sources of bias.
