Proposition: The NYPD is held adequately accountable for civilian complaints

FOR the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • [2] Data Transformations: The dataset was grouped by the year in which each complaint was received, and the total number of unique complaints (complaint_id) were calculated for each year. The number of distinct officers (unique_mos_id) involved in complaints during the same period was then determined. Dividing the total number of complaints by the number of officers each year produced the average number complaints per officer, providing a normalized measure that accounts for fluctuations in the size of the police force over time.
  • [2] Downward Trend Line: A linear trend line was added to highlight the overall decline in average complaints per officer over time, drawing attention to the long-term pattern rather than year-to-year fluctuations.
  • [-1] Truncated Y-Axis: The y-axis begins at 0.8 instead of 0 to visually accentuate the decrease in complaint rates. This design choice makes the downard trend appear steeper, enhancing the visual impression of improvement over time.
  • [2] Dropped Values: Data from 2020 was omitted due to incomplete records, as complaints were collected only during the early months of that year. Including this partial data would have created a misleading spike in the average complaints per officer. Removing it ensures the visualization maintains a consistent comparison across years.
  • [2] Annotations: Text labels were added to mark the peak and lowest points of the trend. These annotations guide viewers toward the most significant moments in the data, reinforcing the main proposition that the NYPD is held accountable for civilian complaints.

AGAINST the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • [2] Dropped 2020 Values: The year 2020 was intentionally excluded from the visualization because complaint data from that year is incomplete, likely as a result of the ongoing reporting period. Including data from that year would have introduced inaccuracies and falsely suggest a sharp decline in both complaints and substantiation rate. By removing this partial data, the visualization maintains a more consistent and fair representation of trends over time.
  • [-2] Framing bias through the title and interpretation: The visualization's title, "Officers Accused of Using Force Rarely Face Consequences," frames the data and conclusion in a negative light. It looks like there is a direct casual relationship between the number of force-related complaints and a low substantiation rate. While persuasive, this framing overlooks that the total number of substantiated comlpaints has increased over time. Moreover, around 1997, there were roughly 30 complaints * 0.4 substantiation rate = 12 substantiated complaints, whereas by 2015, approximately 550 complaints * 0.7 substation rate = 385 substantiated complaints. By focusing only on the ratio rather than the total substantiated outcomes, the visualization makes it seem as though accountability has declined. However, in reality, the number of officers facing consequences has increased, reavealing a deceptive framing that supports the opposing argument.
  • [-1] Selective Framing: The visualization focuses on complaints categorized under "fado_type" == force, ignoring all other possible categories. This selective inclusino amplifies the perception that the NYPD is not held accountable, as it highlightsa specific area of concern. By filtering the data in this way, the visualization limits the viewer's understanding of overll accountability trends across the department. If other complaint types were included, the substantiation rate may appear more balanced, potentially altering the narrative.
  • [-2] Dual-axis Manipulation: The visualization uses two y-axes (Red for total complaints, blue for substantiation rate) to suggest a relationship between metrics that are not directly comparable. This design choice misleads viewers into believing that accountability has declined over time, when the visual difference is merely a result of axes manipulation.

Final Reflection

Our goal for this project was to determine whether the NYPD is held accountable for civilian complaints. While creating these visualizations, there were parts that were straightforward and others were difficult and required careful thought. It was easy to group by year and by officer, compute totals and averages, then produce line plots. However, what was difficult was crafting credible and accurate visualizations for both the supporting and opposing sides when the data did not always match our initial expectations. Thus, we had to explore multiple tables and relationships within the dataset before we found consistent data that could fairly represent each side of the proposition. Balancing clarity, analytical depth, and bias was also challenging because we needed visuals that were impactful without altering the underlying data.

We were surprised by how much ratios clarified the patterns in the data. Looking at the substantiation rate for use-of-force complaints made long-term trends easier to identify and suggested that problems with excessive force have not improved as much as raw counts imply. Moreover, we also learned that a simple line chart with a regression line can be very persuasive. By highlighting the overall trend, the regression line helps viewers focus on the big picture and overall downward trend rather than getting distracted by small year-to-year fluctuations.

Ethical analysis and visualization means telling the truth with data in a way that allows other to reach to reach the same conclusions if they were to analyze the data themselves. Ethical analysis requires showing enough context to make claims credible and verifiable. It also means being transparent about any data cleaning, transformations, or other design choices. The line between "acceptable" persuasive choices and "misleading" choices is whether the design clarifies without hiding or distorting anything. Acceptable choices keep axes, scales, and labels faithful to the data, while misleading choices manipulate these elements to exaggerate or downplay certain aspects. A simple test is whether someone else can use the same data and steps to recreate the visualizations and reach the same conclusion.
