Proposition: The NYPD is held adequately accountable for civilian complaints

FOR the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • [2] Data Transformations: The dataset was grouped by the year in which each complaint was received, and the total number of unique complaints (complaint_id) were calculated for each year. The number of distinct officers (unique_mos_id) involved in complaints during the same period was then determined. Dividing the total number of complaints by the number of officers each year produced the average number complaints per officer, providing a normalized measure that accounts for fluctuations in the size of the police force over time
  • [2] Downward Trend Line: A linear trend line was added to highlight the overall decline in average complaints per officer over time, drawing attention to the long-term pattern rather than year-to-year fluctuations
  • [-1] Truncated Y-Axis: The y-axis begins at 0.8 instead of 0 to visually accentuate the decrease in complaint rates. This design choice makes the downward trend appear steeper, enhancing the visual impression of improvement over time
  • [1.5] Dropped Values: Data from 2020 was omitted due to incomplete records, as complaints were collected only during the early months of that year. Including this partial data would have created a misleading spike in the average complaints per officer. Removing it ensures the visualization maintains a consistent comparison across years
  • [2] Annotations: Text labels were added to mark the peak and lowest points of the trend. These annotations guide viewers toward the most significant moments in the data, reinforcing the main proposition that the NYPD is held accountable for civilian complaints

AGAINST the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • [-0.5] Truncated Y-Axis: The y-axis begins at 0.5 rather than 0 to exaggerate the perceived decline in substantation rates over time. This framing choice visually amplifies small variations, making the downward trend appear steeper and more pronounced than it actually is.
  • [-2] Title Framing: The title employs emotionally charged language to establish a critical tone. By emphasizing that officers "rarely face consequences," the visualization primes viewers to interpret the data as evidence of systemic accountability issues within the NYPD. However, this framing hides contextual information, such as the rising number of total substantiated cases in recent years, which would provide a more balanced perspective
  • [-1] Selective Filtering: The visualization focuses exclusively on complaints involving use of force, excluding other categories. This selective focus narrows the audience's attention to one of the most severe complaint types, heightening the emotional and ethical weight of the visualization. While this strengthens hte persuasive narrative, it presents an invcomplete picture of overall complaint outcomes across the departments

Final Reflection

One surprising aspect of this project was how different data transformations could completely change how trends appeared. For instance, calculating ratios, such as the substantiation rate of force complaints, made long-term patterns much clearer and highlighted how the issue of excessive force has persisted over time. We also discovered that visual form plays a major role in persuasion: combining a line chart with a regression line often produced the most convincing narrative. By emphasizing the overall downward or upward trend, these visuals helped audiences focus on the broader pattern rather than minor year-to-year fluctuations.

Creating persuasive yet ethical visualizations proved to be a challenging balance. While it was relatively easy to compute yearly averages using groupby functions and generate line plots, finding data that convincingly supported either side of our proposition required extensive exploration. In many cases, the data did not align with our initial expectations causing many road bumps, forcing us to rethink how to best represent it. The most difficult aspect was maintaining analytical depth and clarity without oversimplifying complex patterns or creating misleading narratives through poor design choices. Subtle visual techniques, such as axis scaling or selective filtering, could make trends appear more dramatic than they truly were, reminding us how easily data can be manipulated to fit a desired story.

Through this process, we came to define ethical analysis and visualization as the effort to present data truthfully, without ignoring unfavorable information or reinforcing bias. Ethical visualization means helping audiences correctly interpret the data's meaning without being influenced by misleading design choices. Techniques like truncating axes, using emotionally charged titles, or selectively filtering data can all skew perception, so maintaining transparency about these decisions is essential to establish trust. An ethical visualization should reveal the full context behind the data, acknowledging its limitations and potential sources of bias.
